The messenger in the Jellyfish Seas


Noah lived on a boat that was attached to a tree with a long metal chain, only a twenty-minute walk away from the beach. The tree was on the property of a good friend of his, thus he lived rent free. This was a blessing since Noah only had to get dinner on the table and pay a small fee for electricity to his friend.





On his way to the beach, he usually got a cup of Kudzu blossom tea – not that there where many other affordable options –. Ever since the blooms became common, import products have seen a steep rise in prizes. His usual spot was a food truck of which the engine broke down. The owner was getting old and had decided to build some wooden walls around it with a corrugated sheet on top, just to give costumers some basic shelter. The owner had also decorated the shack with a combination of small, porcelain, geisha statues that were scattered around the establishment, seemingly random, and surprisingly high-quality pictures of jellyfish that where carefully framed and hung on the walls. He wasn’t sure about the geisha statues, but he could appreciate the pictures. During the evening shady folk would come, but during early mornings – which is usually when Noah passed by – it tended to be nearly deserted, maybe someone with business in the area but the Pueraria montana (Kudzu) made it hard to see the shack from a distance. Noah liked this shack, because from here you had the best view over the thousands of jellyfish that gathered in the bay.

“Morning!” Noah said whilst leaning his surfboard against the wall, “Can I have a Kudzu Blossom tea, please?” The owner mumbled something about early mornings and asked if he wanted the usual Honeysuckle blend. Noah confirmed, seated himself next to the makeshift window and looked at the other-worldly spectacle in the water. The economic damage of the jellyfish was nearly immeasurable.
“The global spread of jellyfish hazards mirrors the pace of human imprint in the marine environment” - Sun-Hee Lee, Li-Chun Tseng, Yang Ho Yoon, Eduardo Ramirez-Romero, Jiang-Shiou Hwang, Juan Carlos Molinero (2023)

There had been countless warning signs, but it was simply never profitable enough to do something about it, and where would one start anyway?

Within this system without end, the ones with capital will act to preserve their growth and are able to control the narrative with their resources. Ten minutes later he arrived at another shack, although more professionally put together, clearly not meant to be there for an extended period. There was a sticker on the window that said: “Splash Delivery”. He walked in, greeted a young man behind the counter and asked for a new job. There was a document that had to be delivered to Ella on the big island (Hawaii).

The Splash Delivery service was a niche electric boat/surfboard company that would still be able to deliver small important packages and documents on a wind still day, in the middle of the bloom season. One of the perks of working for Splash Delivery was that you received an electric board that you could keep for as long as they worked there, which was the initial reason Noah had been drawn to the profession. A profession that thanked its whole existence to the blooms, as they made traditional means of sending packages overseas nearly impossible when they happened.

Getting the job had been surprisingly easy, as his reputation from the local surf spot had proceeded him. The only hassle had been a flyer about the company, which they had asked him to memorize. Although, primarily a pretty façade, riddled with greenwashing, one paragraph had always stuck with him. A paragraph copied from a study on the relation between human influence and jellyfish populations. “Temporal fluctuations in abundance and size of the giant jellyfish Nemopilema nomurai medusae in the northern East China Sea, 2006–2017” - Satoshi Kitajima, Toru Hasegawa, Koh Nishiuchi, Yoko Kiyomoto, Takeshi Taneda & Haruya Yamada (2020)

As he reached the shore, the familiar sight of algae puddles, plastic thrash and washed-up jellyfish greeted him.

Looking down on the jellyfish that were floating around him, he spotted some Blue Sea Dragons (Glaucus Atlanticus). Now that the sea was filled to the brim with their favorite food, the little sea dragons could eat as much as they wanted. They lay their eggs on the abundance of floating plastic, carried by the ocean currents from the Pacific Garbage Patch to the seas surrounding Hawaii. Noah always felt like there was something to be learned from these organisms, the way they let themselves get carried by the current, only changing their course when necessary, making the best out of their given circumstances. Next to the sea slug, there are others that benefited from the excess of plastic in the oceans. The goat herder in the Kudzu Fields




Ella was staring at her tracking device with a stern face, it had never happened before that she had permanently lost a goat from her herd. One goat wondered off especially far, this stressed her out, as she had promised to lead the heard to the land of a new client today, and atop of all that, she had also ordered a new map. Since she didn’t have a permanent residence, she had to wait for the Splash Delivery guy at the beach around eight in the evening. All of this went through her mind as the blinking red dot moved slowly towards the north; at least that was in the right direction.





She had been doing this job for five years now, traveling from farm to farm on the big island, letting her goats eat the Kudzu that was threatening to flood the farmlands. Sometimes she would sleep in the house or a shed of the farmers. When the weather was nice, she preferred to camp in a tent close to the goats amidst the Kudzu. Especially during the late summer, when the Kudzu blooms and their purple flowers released their grape-like scent. A tentacular organism that changed her country up to the chemical composition of the soil. Not just drowning the native vegetation in its leaves, but also creating soil that favors the displaced over the native. The Kudzu is what they call a Nitrogen fixing plant, that sounds lovely of course, but as the native species have adapted for a harsher environment, they are not well equipped to make use of this abundance of nutrition, and they get outcompeted by unwanted guests.“Temporal fluctuations in abundance and size of the giant jellyfish Nemopilema nomurai medusae in the northern East China Sea, 2006–2017” - Satoshi Kitajima, Toru Hasegawa, Koh Nishiuchi, Yoko Kiyomoto, Takeshi Taneda & Haruya Yamada (2020) Ella’s only solace was the fact that the Kudzu’s potential habitat range on the big island (which is the last refuge for many of the remaining native species) has primarily been occupied by invasive land cover for over a hundred years. Ella:
Most of Hawaii’s “native” plant species came from the seas, seeds drifting from the continents and finding their way here. Sometimes new species would disrupt, and sometimes they would bring relative balance. Together with the birds and the wind, the sea brought the ancestors to almost all of Hawaii’s unique species, slowly growing and shifting the ecosystem. But nowadays the bird populations dwindle and the sea only brings plastic. Meanwhile humans are spreading plants around the world accidentally, or for the simple reason that they look pretty in their botanical gardens or on their lawn. Our ecological memory is fading and with it, nature’s resilience to deal with all of their invaders. If we don’t help our environment adapt to changes set in motion a long time ago, we will lose more biodiversity than our species will ever witness again. The change you are speaking of however, is already here. Do not underestimate what we have learned in the face of this catastrophe, and how many dedicated people use this knowledge, to provoke change. You might not have helped the sea directly, but you are already part of a new wave. Carrying a little bit of the ocean’s burden, by taking on a part of her role, she was no longer able to fulfil. Nudging our ecosystem in the right direction, by helping me spread this small, yellow butterfly.
